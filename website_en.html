Welcome to the First Ultrasound Tomography Imaging Conference

The "First Ultrasound Tomography Imaging Conference" co-hosted by the College of Mathematics, Physics and Medicine at Zhejiang Normal University, the College of Life Science and Technology at Huazhong University of Science and Technology, the Hubei Province High-end Biomedical Imaging Major Scientific Infrastructure, and the Hubei Province Biomedical Engineering Society will be held at Zhejiang Normal University from October 24 to 26, 2025.

This symposium aims to promote academic exchanges in fields related to ultrasound tomography imaging and inverse problem solving mathematical theories and methods, and to discuss the latest research progress and clinical applications in this field.

About the Conference

Ultrasound Tomography Imaging is an emerging medical imaging technology that reconstructs the acoustic parameter distribution of tissues by measuring the propagation characteristics of ultrasound waves in tissues. Compared to traditional ultrasound imaging, ultrasound tomography imaging can provide more quantitative and accurate diagnostic information.

This technology is based on the differences in propagation speed, attenuation, and scattering characteristics of ultrasound waves in different media. By transmitting and receiving ultrasound signals from multiple angles, advanced mathematical algorithms are used to reconstruct the two-dimensional or three-dimensional distribution images of parameters such as sound speed, density, and attenuation coefficient of the tissues. This quantitative imaging method overcomes the shortcomings of traditional ultrasound imaging, such as strong subjectivity and difficulty in standardization.

Ultrasound tomography imaging shows great potential in clinical applications such as breast cancer screening, liver fibrosis assessment, and bone density measurement. Especially in the early diagnosis of breast cancer, this technology can provide high-contrast images similar to MRI while avoiding radiation exposure and high costs, with important clinical value and development prospects.

This conference will bring together experts and scholars in the field of ultrasound tomography imaging from home and abroad to discuss the latest research progress, mathematical theories and methods, computational imaging algorithms, and clinical application prospects of this technology.

Talk: Ultrasound Electrical Impedance Tomography: Background, Models and Algorithms

Abstract: In recent years, interdisciplinary research, particularly the integration of science, engineering, and medicine, has played an increasingly important role in clinical diagnosis and treatment. Biomedical tissue image reconstruction, exemplified by technologies such as CT, EIT, MRI, and ultrasound imaging, is a crucial medical imaging technique. This technology generally involves using externally measurable information to non-invasively (or with minimal invasion) probe the internal structure or abnormal changes of human tissues, thereby assisting doctors in clinical diagnosis and treatment. The core technology involves a cross-disciplinary integration of biology, physics, chemistry, engineering, and mathematics, with imaging models and algorithms based on mathematical-physical inverse problems being the fundamental underlying technology. Electrical Impedance Tomography (EIT), due to its relatively simple data acquisition and non-radioactive nature, has found preliminary applications in clinical fields such as lung disease monitoring, brain function imaging, and cancer diagnosis. However, existing traditional EIT equipment, which only collects boundary current-potential information of biological tissues, suffers from low spatial resolution. In recent years, ultrasound-EIT, which fuses ultrasound information with EIT imaging models, has garnered significant academic attention. The basic principle is that ultrasound waves of a certain frequency emitted from outside the tissue cause changes in the internal conductivity, indirectly providing energy information at various points within the biological tissue, thus enabling more stable and efficient detection of electrical impedance. In this talk, we will introduce the medical background, physical principles, and basic data analysis techniques of biological tissue electrical impedance imaging, as well as the models and algorithms for ultrasound-EIT.

Talk: Research on Ring-Array Ultrasound Reflection Tomography and System Development

Abstract: Ring-array ultrasound reflection tomography utilizes a ring-array probe to achieve 360° omnidirectional focusing, which can not only significantly improve the resolution of reflection images but also enable automatic 3D imaging of the breast, thus having potential application value in breast cancer screening. However, due to the large number of elements and complex structure of the ring-array system, as well as the relatively insufficient research on its beamforming direction, there are still many challenges. To address these issues, this report's research work mainly includes three aspects: first, studying a phase error correction method based on sound speed images and systematically analyzing the impact of different resolution sound speed images on the correction effect; second, exploring a fast sound speed image reconstruction method based on deep learning to improve imaging efficiency; and third, introducing the development progress of our team's breast ultrasound tomography system and looking forward to the next stage of research plans.

Talk: Ultrasound Speed-of-Sound Tomography Based on Implicit Neural Representation

Abstract: Ultrasound Speed-of-Sound Tomography (USCT) can capture the sound speed contrast difference between breast tumors and surrounding soft tissues, showing significant promise in the early screening of breast cancer. However, existing USCT image reconstruction methods generally suffer from a highly ill-posed inverse problem, leading to unstable image reconstruction and image quality that is susceptible to measurement errors. To address these issues, this study explores an ultrasound speed-of-sound tomography method based on Implicit Neural Representation (INR). In bent-ray tracing USCT, we combine Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) with a BentRay propagation model, using a multi-layer perceptron to model the slowness field as a continuous function, proposing the BentRay-NeRF method. This method significantly outperforms the traditional Gauss-Newton method in terms of artifact suppression and image reconstruction stability. In Full-Waveform Inversion (FWI) USCT, to tackle the ill-posedness and cycle-skipping problems of the reconstruction, we propose the INR-FWI method. This method also uses a multi-layer perceptron to model the sound speed field as a continuous function and introduces implicit regularization constraints to ensure the smoothness of the solution space. In an unsupervised framework, it directly uses measured ultrasound waveform signals for sound speed image reconstruction, avoiding the reliance of traditional supervised methods on large-scale training data. In numerical simulation experiments and in-vivo human breast data, INR-FWI demonstrates stronger robustness and better imaging quality compared to the traditional conjugate gradient FWI method.

Talk: 3D Ultrasound Tomography at KIT: Current status

Abstract: The Karlsruhe Institute of Technology (KIT) is developing 3D Ultrasound Computed Tomography (3D USCT) for breast imaging. Unlike 2D USCT systems, 3D USCT enables fully volumetric, high-resolution imaging with nearly isotropic spatial resolution. This is achieved through a hemispherical arrangement of thousands of transducers that simultaneously capture reflection, speed of sound, and attenuation data. The current prototype, 3D USCT III, integrates custom transducer arrays, high-throughput analog/digital electronics, and GPU-accelerated reconstruction algorithms. It is the first true 3D USCT and was recently undergoing clinical validation at Vall d’Hebron University Hospital in Barcelona. By offering non-ionizing, reproducible, and patient-friendly imaging, 3D USCT has the potential to complement or even replace mammography and MRI in specific clinical scenarios, especially in early breast cancer detection.

Talk: Biomedical Inverse Scattering Imaging Based on Neurophysical Simulation

Abstract: High-resolution imaging of deep biological tissues has long been constrained by the computational bottlenecks of strong scattering and nonlinear propagation. Neural PDE solvers, with their efficiency and stability in approximating complex wave PDEs, are poised to become powerful tools for inverse scattering image reconstruction. However, limited by the scarcity of biomedical data and the insufficient generalization capabilities of existing neural operator networks, their imaging performance remains unsatisfactory. This report will introduce a novel inverse scattering solving framework based on neurophysical simulation and report its experimental results on full-wave ultrasound tomography. This method combines the high precision of traditional PDE physical modeling with the computational efficiency and stability of neural networks. It can achieve 3D ultrasound tomography reconstruction of human tissue mechanical parameters with a resolution comparable to MRI (about 1 mm) in about ten minutes, providing a new quantitative assessment solution for the clinical diagnosis of sports injuries and tumors. This framework can also be extended to optical diffraction tomography of thick samples such as organoids and embryos, opening up new avenues for cell biology research.

Talk: A modified Newton method for cracks from the far field patterns with a fixed incident direction

Abstract: We consider the inverse cracks scattering problems from the far field patterns with a fixed incident direction. We firstly show that the sound-soft cracks can be uniquely determined by the multi-frequency far field patterns with a fixed incident direction. The proof is based on a low frequency asymptotic analysis of the scattered field. One important feature of the uniqueness result is that the background can even be an unknown inhomogeneous medium.A modified Newton method is then proposed for the numerical reconstruction of the shapes and locations of the cracks. Compared to the classical Newton method, the modified Newton method relaxes the dependence of a good initial guess and can be applied for multiple cracks. Numerical examples in two dimensions are presented to demonstrate the feasibility and effectiveness of the modified Newton method. In particular, the quality of the reconstructions can be greatly improved if we use the measurements properly with two frequencies or two incident directions.

Talk: Starter-Iterator inspired Neural Operator for Solving Forward and Inverse Problems

Abstract: Operator learning is an interdisciplinary field that combines machine learning with scientific computing to construct efficient mappings between input and output function spaces, thereby reducing the computational complexity of solving high-dimensional partial differential equations. We introduce a novel neural operator framework, the Starter-Iterator inspired Neural Operator (SINO), which reimagines the initialization strategies and iteration formats of traditional methods using neural networks. This leads to a new paradigm for spectral-spatiotemporal collaborative modeling: frequency domain learning captures globally stable low-frequency features, while time domain learning refines local solution residuals, overcoming the limitations of traditional single-domain approaches. Extensive experiments on dynamic systems, including the Navier-Stokes and acoustic wave equations, demonstrate that SINO delivers exceptional numerical accuracy, generalization capability, and robustness.

Talk: High-Resolution Full-Wave Ultrasound Tomography: Methods and Applications

Abstract: Full-waveform inversion is a high-resolution acoustic parameter model reconstruction and imaging method based on optimization theory. Traditional B-mode ultrasound imaging methods, which use multiple sets of self-excited and self-received pulse-echo signals, rely on uniform speed and ray assumptions to reconstruct grayscale images of the target's structure, resulting in limited resolution, difficulty in identifying small lesions, and poor imaging performance in the presence of hard tissues with large sound speed differences, such as bones. This study adopts a surrounding ring-array full-matrix observation method to fully collect information from reflected, scattered, refracted, and transmitted waves. It uses a full-waveform inversion method based on a multi-parameter wave equation (sound speed, acoustic impedance, attenuation, etc.) to reconstruct the optimal numerical parameter model that can explain the observed signals. The results have a spatial resolution of millimeters or even sub-millimeters. Through numerical simulations, acoustic phantoms, ex-vivo tissue, and in-vivo experimental tests, it was found that the full-waveform inversion method successfully reconstructed high-resolution acoustic parameter models. The results are in good agreement with magnetic resonance imaging, fully verifying the robustness and effectiveness of the method.
